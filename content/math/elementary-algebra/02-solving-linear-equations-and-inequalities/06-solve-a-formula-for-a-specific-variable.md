## Use the Distance, Rate, and Time Formula

One formula you will use often in algebra and in everyday life is the
formula for distance traveled by an object moving at a constant rate. Rate
is an equivalent word for "speed." Do you know what distance you travel if
you drive at a steady rate of $60$ miles per hour for $2$ hours? (This might
happen if you use your car's cruise control while driving on the highway.)
If you said $120$ miles, you already know how to use this formula!

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Distance, Rate, and Time.** For an object moving at a uniform (constant)
  rate, the distance traveled, the elapsed time, and the rate are related by
  the formula

  $$d = rt$$

  where $d = $ distance, $r = $ rate, and $t = $ time.
{{< /callout >}}

We will use the Strategy for Solving Applications introduced earlier in
this chapter. When the problem requires a formula, we change Step 4: in
place of writing a sentence, we write the appropriate formula.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Solve an application (with a formula).**

  1. **Read** the problem. Make sure all the words and ideas are understood.
  2. **Identify** what we are looking for.
  3. **Name** what we are looking for. Choose a variable to represent that
     quantity.
  4. **Translate** into an equation. Write the appropriate formula for the
     situation. Substitute in the given information.
  5. **Solve** the equation using good algebra techniques.
  6. **Check** the answer in the problem and make sure it makes sense.
  7. **Answer** the question with a complete sentence.
{{< /callout >}}

You may want to create a mini-chart to summarize the information in the
problem, as in the first example below.

**Example.** Jamal rides his bike at a uniform rate of $12$ miles per hour
for $3\tfrac{1}{2}$ hours. What distance has he traveled?

| Step | |
| :--- | :--- |
| Step 1. Read the problem. | |
| Step 2. Identify what we are looking for. | distance traveled |
| Step 3. Name. Choose a variable. | Let $d = $ distance. |
| Step 4. Translate. Write the formula, with $r = 12$ mph, $t = 3\tfrac{1}{2}$ hours. | $d = rt$ |
| Substitute in the given information. | $d = 12 \cdot 3\tfrac{1}{2}$ |
| Step 5. Solve the equation. | $d = 42$ miles |
| Step 6. Check. Does $42$ miles make sense? Jamal rides $12$ miles in $1$ hour, $24$ miles in $2$ hours, $36$ miles in $3$ hours — so $42$ miles in $3\tfrac{1}{2}$ hours is reasonable. | |
| Step 7. Answer the question with a complete sentence. | Jamal rode $42$ miles. |

**Example.** Rey is planning to drive from his house in San Diego to visit
his grandmother in Sacramento, a distance of $520$ miles. If he can drive at
a steady rate of $65$ miles per hour, how many hours will the trip take?

We identify that we are looking for time, so let $t = $ time. We know
$d = 520$ miles and $r = 65$ mph.

$$d = rt \qquad\qquad 520 = 65t$$

Dividing both sides by $65$ gives $t = 8$. We check by substituting back
into the formula: $520 \overset{?}{=} 65 \cdot 8$, and indeed
$520 = 520$. ✓ Rey's trip will take $8$ hours.

## Solve a Formula for a Specific Variable

You are probably familiar with some geometry formulas. A formula is a
mathematical description of the relationship between variables. Formulas
are also used in the sciences, such as chemistry, physics, and biology. In
medicine they are used for calculations for dispensing medicine or
determining body mass index. Spreadsheet programs rely on formulas to make
calculations. It is important to be familiar with formulas and be able to
manipulate them easily.

In the two examples above, we used the formula $d = rt$. This formula gives
the value of $d$, distance, when you substitute in the values of $r$ and
$t$. But in the second example, we had to find the value of $t$ — we
substituted values of $d$ and $r$ and then used algebra to solve for $t$. If
you had to do this often, you might wonder why there is not a formula that
gives the value of $t$ directly when you substitute in the values of $d$
and $r$. We can make a formula like this by solving the formula $d = rt$
for $t$.

To **solve a formula for a specific variable** means to isolate that
variable on one side of the equals sign with a coefficient of $1$. All
other variables and constants are on the other side of the equals sign. To
see how to solve a formula for a specific variable, we will start with the
distance, rate, and time formula.

**Example.** Solve the formula $d = rt$ for $t$:
(a) when $d = 520$ and $r = 65$ (b) in general.

We write the solutions side-by-side to demonstrate that solving a formula
in general uses the same steps as when we have numbers to substitute.

| | (a) when $d = 520$ and $r = 65$ | (b) in general |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Write the formula. | $d = rt$ | $d = rt$ |
| Substitute. | $520 = 65t$ | |
| Divide, to isolate $t$. | $\tfrac{520}{65} = \tfrac{65t}{65}$ | $\tfrac{d}{r} = \tfrac{rt}{r}$ |
| Simplify. | $8 = t$ | $\tfrac{d}{r} = t$ |

We say the formula $t = \tfrac{d}{r}$ is solved for $t$.

  answer="50"
  hint="Substitute $d = 315$ and $t = 6.3$ into $d = rt$, then divide both sides by t to isolate r."
>}}

**Example.** Solve the formula $A = \tfrac{1}{2}bh$ for $h$:
(a) when $A = 90$ and $b = 15$ (b) in general.

| | (a) when $A = 90$ and $b = 15$ | (b) in general |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Write the formula. | $A = \tfrac{1}{2}bh$ | $A = \tfrac{1}{2}bh$ |
| Substitute. | $90 = \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 15 \cdot h$ | |
| Clear the fractions (multiply both sides by $2$). | $2 \cdot 90 = 2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2} \cdot 15h$ | $2 \cdot A = 2 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}bh$ |
| Simplify. | $180 = 15h$ | $2A = bh$ |
| Solve for $h$. | $12 = h$ | $\tfrac{2A}{b} = h$ |

We can now find the height of a triangle, if we know the area and the base,
by using the formula $h = \tfrac{2A}{b}$.

The formula $I = Prt$ is used to calculate simple interest, $I$, for a
principal, $P$, invested at rate, $r$, for $t$ years.

**Example.** Solve the formula $I = Prt$ to find the principal, $P$:
(a) when $I = \text{\textdollar}5{,}600$, $r = 4\%$, $t = 7$ years (b) in general.

| | (a) when $I = \text{\textdollar}5{,}600$, $r = 4\%$, $t = 7$ years | (b) in general |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Write the formula. | $I = Prt$ | $I = Prt$ |
| Substitute. | $5600 = P(0.04)(7)$ | |
| Simplify. | $5600 = P(0.28)$ | $I = P(rt)$ |
| Divide, to isolate $P$. | $\tfrac{5600}{0.28} = \tfrac{P(0.28)}{0.28}$ | $\tfrac{I}{rt} = \tfrac{P(rt)}{rt}$ |
| Simplify. | $20{,}000 = P$ | $\tfrac{I}{rt} = P$ |

The principal is $\text{\textdollar}20{,}000$.

  question="Use the formula I = Prt to find the principal, P, in general."
  answer="\frac{I}{rt}"
  hint="Simplify the right side to P(rt), then divide both sides by rt."
>}}

Later in this class, and in future algebra classes, you'll encounter
equations that relate two variables, usually $x$ and $y$. You might be
given an equation that is solved for $y$ and need to solve it for $x$, or
vice versa. In the following example, we're given an equation with both $x$
and $y$ on the same side and we'll solve it for $y$.

**Example.** Solve the formula $3x + 2y = 18$ for $y$:
(a) when $x = 4$ (b) in general.

| | (a) when $x = 4$ | (b) in general |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| | $3x + 2y = 18$ | $3x + 2y = 18$ |
| Substitute. | $3(4) + 2y = 18$ | |
| Subtract to isolate the $y$-term. | $12 - 12 + 2y = 18 - 12$ | $3x - 3x + 2y = 18 - 3x$ |
| Divide. | $\tfrac{2y}{2} = \tfrac{6}{2}$ | $\tfrac{2y}{2} = \tfrac{18 - 3x}{2}$ |
| Simplify. | $y = 3$ | $y = -\tfrac{3x}{2} + 9$ |

{{< fillin
  question="Solve the formula $3x + 4y = 10$ for y, in general."
  answer="\frac{10 - 3x}{4}"
  hint="Subtract 3x from both sides first, then divide every term by 4."
>}}

In the examples above, we used the numbers in part (a) as a guide to
solving in general in part (b). Now we will solve a formula in general
without using numbers as a guide.

**Example.** Solve the formula $P = a + b + c$ for $a$.

We isolate $a$ on one side of the equation. Both $b$ and $c$ are added to
$a$, so we subtract them from both sides of the equation.

$$P = a + b + c$$
$$P - b - c = a + b + c - b - c$$
$$P - b - c = a$$
$$a = P - b - c$$

{{< fillin
  question="Solve the formula $P = a + b + c$ for b."
  answer="P - a - c"
  hint="Subtract a and c from both sides to isolate b."
>}}

**Example.** Solve the formula $6x + 5y = 13$ for $y$.

$$6x + 5y = 13$$

Subtract $6x$ from both sides to isolate the term with $y$:

$$6x - 6x + 5y = 13 - 6x$$
$$5y = 13 - 6x$$

Divide by $5$ to make the coefficient of $y$ equal to $1$:

$$\frac{5y}{5} = \frac{13 - 6x}{5}$$
$$y = \frac{13 - 6x}{5}$$

The fraction is already simplified — we cannot divide $13 - 6x$ by $5$,
since $5$ does not divide evenly into both terms of the numerator.

  hint="Subtract 4x from both sides first, then divide every term by 7."
>}}

Formulas from geometry can be solved for a specific variable the same way.
Some familiar ones are the perimeter of a rectangle, $P = 2L + 2W$, the
circumference of a circle, $C = \pi d$, and the volume of a rectangular
solid, $V = LWH$.

## Key terms

**rate** — an equivalent word for speed; how fast an object moves per unit
of time. **solve a formula for a specific variable** — to isolate that
variable on one side of the equals sign, with a coefficient of $1$, while
all other variables and constants are on the other side.
