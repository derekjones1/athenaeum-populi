## Use Variables and Algebraic Symbols

Suppose this year Greg is $20$ years old and Alex is $23$. Alex is always
$3$ years older than Greg — when Greg was $12$, Alex was $15$; when Greg is
$35$, Alex will be $38$. No matter what Greg's age is, Alex's age is always
$3$ more. In the language of algebra, we say that Greg's age and Alex's age
are **variables** and the $3$ is a **constant**. The ages change ("vary")
but the $3$ years between them always stays the same ("constant").

In algebra, we use letters of the alphabet to represent variables. If we
call Greg's age $g$, then Alex's age is $g + 3$.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Variable.** A **variable** is a letter that represents a number whose
  value may change.

  **Constant.** A **constant** is a number whose value always stays the
  same.
{{< /callout >}}

The letters most commonly used for variables are $x$, $y$, $a$, $b$, and
$c$. To write algebraically, we need operation symbols as well as numbers
and variables.

| Operation | Notation | Say | The result is |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Addition | $a + b$ | $a$ plus $b$ | the sum of $a$ and $b$ |
| Subtraction | $a - b$ | $a$ minus $b$ | the difference of $a$ and $b$ |
| Multiplication | $a \cdot b,\ ab,\ a(b),\ (a)(b)$ | $a$ times $b$ | the product of $a$ and $b$ |
| Division | $a \div b,\ a/b,\ \tfrac{a}{b},\ b)\overline{a}$ | $a$ divided by $b$ | the quotient of $a$ and $b$ ($a$ is the dividend, $b$ is the divisor) |

When translating between symbols and English, pay attention to the words
"of" and "and": the *difference of* $9$ and $2$ means $9 - 2$; the
*product of* $4$ and $8$ means $4 \cdot 8$. In algebra, the cross symbol
$\times$ is not used for multiplication, because it can be confused with
the variable $x$ — does $3xy$ mean $3 \times y$ ("three times $y$") or
$3 \cdot x \cdot y$ ("three times $x$ times $y$")? Using $\cdot$ or
parentheses keeps it clear.

When two quantities have the same value, we connect them with an **equal
sign**: $a = b$ is read "$a$ is equal to $b$."

On the number line, numbers get larger from left to right, which explains
the symbols $<$ and $>$: $a < b$ ("$a$ is less than $b$") means $a$ is to
the left of $b$ on the number line, and $a > b$ ("$a$ is greater than
$b$") means $a$ is to the right of $b$. The statement $a < b$ is
equivalent to $b > a$ — for example, $7 < 11$ is equivalent to $11 > 7$.

| Symbol | Words |
| :--- | :--- |
| $a \neq b$ | $a$ is not equal to $b$ |
| $a < b$ | $a$ is less than $b$ |
| $a \le b$ | $a$ is less than or equal to $b$ |
| $a > b$ | $a$ is greater than $b$ |
| $a \ge b$ | $a$ is greater than or equal to $b$ |

**Example.** Translate from algebra into English: (a) $17 \le 26$
(b) $8 \neq 17 - 3$ (c) $12 > 27 \div 3$ (d) $y + 7 < 19$.

(a) $17$ is less than or equal to $26$. (b) $8$ is not equal to $17$ minus
$3$. (c) $12$ is greater than $27$ divided by $3$. (d) $y$ plus $7$ is less
than $19$.

Grouping symbols — **parentheses** $(\ )$, **brackets** $[\ ]$, and
**braces** $\{\ \}$ — work like punctuation marks in English: they show
which expressions belong together. For example, $8(14 - 8)$,
$21 - 3[2 + 4(9 - 8)]$, and $24 \div \{13 - 2[1(6-5) + 4]\}$ all use
grouping symbols; we'll simplify expressions like these later in this
section.

A phrase expresses a single thought that is incomplete by itself, but a
sentence makes a complete statement with a subject and a verb. In algebra,
an **expression** is like a phrase, and an **equation** is like a
sentence.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Expression.** An **expression** is a number, a variable, or a
  combination of numbers and variables using operation symbols.

  **Equation.** An **equation** is two expressions connected by an equal
  sign.
{{< /callout >}}

For example, $3 + 5$ ("$3$ plus $5$," the sum of three and five) is an
expression, while $3 + 5 = 8$ ("the sum of three and five is equal to
eight") is an equation — the equal sign gives it a verb, making it a
complete sentence.

**Example.** Determine if each is an expression or an equation:
(a) $2(x + 3) = 10$ (b) $4(y - 1) + 1$ (c) $x \div 25$ (d) $y + 8 = 40$.

(a) is an *equation* — two expressions are connected with an equal sign.
(b) is an *expression* — no equal sign. (c) is an *expression* — no equal
sign. (d) is an *equation* — two expressions are connected with an equal
sign.

Suppose we need to multiply $2$ by itself nine times. Writing
$2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2$ is
tedious, so instead we use **exponents**: $2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2$ is written
$2^3$, and $2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2$
is written $2^9$. In an expression like $2^3$, the $2$ is called the
**base** and the $3$ is the **exponent** — it tells us how many times to
multiply the base by itself. We read $2^3$ as "two to the third power" or
"two cubed."

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Exponential notation.** $a^n$ means multiply $a$ by itself $n$ times:
  $$a^n = \underbrace{a \cdot a \cdot a \cdots a}_{n \text{ factors}}$$
  The expression $a^n$ is read "$a$ to the $n^{\text{th}}$ power."
{{< /callout >}}

While we read $a^n$ as "$a$ to the $n^{\text{th}}$ power," we usually read
$a^2$ as "$a$ squared" and $a^3$ as "$a$ cubed" — you'll see later why
these two have special names.

**Example.** Simplify $3^4$. Expand: $3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3$. Multiply
left to right: $9 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 = 27 \cdot 3 = 81$.

## Simplify Expressions Using the Order of Operations

To **simplify an expression** means to do all the operations in it. For
example, to simplify $4 \cdot 2 + 1$, we multiply first to get $8$, then
add $1$ to get $9$. A good habit is to work down the page, writing each
step below the previous one, and never use an equal sign when simplifying
an expression — that helps avoid confusing expressions with equations.

Consider $4 + 3 \cdot 7$. Some students say $49$ (add first, then
multiply: $7 \cdot 7 = 49$); others say $25$ (multiply first, then add:
$4 + 21 = 25$). The same expression should always give the same result,
so mathematicians established the **Order of Operations**:

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Perform the order of operations.**

  1. **Parentheses and other grouping symbols** — simplify all expressions
     inside grouping symbols, working from the innermost set outward.
  2. **Exponents** — simplify all expressions with exponents.
  3. **Multiplication and division** — perform these in order from left to
     right; they have equal priority.
  4. **Addition and subtraction** — perform these in order from left to
     right; they have equal priority.
{{< /callout >}}

A common mnemonic takes the first letter of each step: "**P**lease
**E**xcuse **M**y **D**ear **A**unt **S**ally" (Parentheses, Exponents,
Multiplication/Division, Addition/Subtraction). "My Dear" reminds us that
multiplication and division have equal priority — we don't always multiply
before dividing or always divide before multiplying, we work left to
right. Likewise "Aunt Sally" reminds us that addition and subtraction have
equal priority and are also done left to right.

**Example.** Simplify (a) $4 + 3 \cdot 7$ (b) $(4+3) \cdot 7$.

(a) No parentheses, no exponents, but there is multiplication: multiply
first to get $4 + 21$, then add to get $25$.

(b) There are parentheses: simplify inside them first to get $(7) \cdot 7$,
then multiply to get $49$. Notice grouping symbols change the result.

**Example.** Simplify $18 \div 6 + 4(5-2)$.

Parentheses first: $18 \div 6 + 4(3)$. No exponents. Multiplication and
division, left to right — divide first since it comes first: $3 + 4(3)$,
then multiply: $3 + 12$. Finally add: $15$.

**Example.** Simplify $5 + 2^3 + 3[6 - 3(4-2)]$.

When there are multiple grouping symbols, simplify the innermost first and
work outward. Focus on the parentheses inside the brackets: $4 - 2 = 2$,
giving $5 + 2^3 + 3[6 - 3(2)]$. Continue inside the brackets: multiply
$3(2) = 6$, giving $5 + 2^3 + 3[6-6]$, then subtract to get
$5 + 2^3 + 3[0]$ — the brackets need no further simplification. Now the
exponent: $2^3 = 8$, giving $5 + 8 + 3[0]$. Multiply: $5 + 8 + 0$. Add:
$13 + 0 = 13$.

## Evaluate an Expression

To **evaluate an expression** means to find its value when the variable is
replaced by a given number. Substitute the number for the variable, then
follow the order of operations to simplify.

**Example.** Evaluate $7x - 4$ when (a) $x = 5$ and (b) $x = 1$.

(a) $7(5) - 4 = 35 - 4 = 31$. (b) $7(1) - 4 = 7 - 4 = 3$.

**Example.** Evaluate for $x = 4$: (a) $x^2$ (b) $3^x$.

(a) Replace $x$ with $4$: $4^2 = 4 \cdot 4 = 16$.

(b) Replace $x$ with $4$: $3^4 = 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 = 81$.

**Example.** Evaluate $2x^2 + 3x + 8$ when $x = 4$.

Substitute $x = 4$: $2(4)^2 + 3(4) + 8$. Follow the order of operations —
exponent first: $2(16) + 3(4) + 8$. Multiply: $32 + 12 + 8$. Add: $52$.

## Identify and Combine Like Terms

Algebraic expressions are made up of **terms**. A term is a constant, or
the product of a constant and one or more variables — examples include
$7$, $y$, $5x^2$, $9a$, and $b^5$. The constant that multiplies the
variable is called the **coefficient**. Think of the coefficient as the
number in front of the variable: the coefficient of $3x$ is $3$. When we
write $x$ alone, the coefficient is $1$, since $x = 1 \cdot x$.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Term.** A **term** is a constant, or the product of a constant and one
  or more variables.

  **Coefficient.** The **coefficient** of a term is the constant that
  multiplies the variable in the term.
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** Identify the coefficient of each: (a) $14y$ (b) $15x^2$
(c) $a$.

(a) The coefficient of $14y$ is $14$. (b) The coefficient of $15x^2$ is
$15$. (c) The coefficient of $a$ is $1$, since $a = 1 \cdot a$.

Some terms share common traits. Consider the six terms $5x$, $7$, $n^2$,
$4$, $3x$, $9n^2$: $7$ and $4$ are both constants; $5x$ and $3x$ are both
terms with $x$; $n^2$ and $9n^2$ are both terms with $n^2$. When two terms
are constants, or have the same variable raised to the same power, we say
they are **like terms**.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Like terms.** Terms that are either constants or have the same
  variables raised to the same powers are called **like terms**.
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** Identify the like terms: $y^3, 7x^2, 14, 23, 4y^3, 9x, 5x^2$.

$y^3$ and $4y^3$ are like terms — same variable and exponent. $7x^2$ and
$5x^2$ are like terms — same variable and exponent. $14$ and $23$ are like
terms — both constants. There is no other term like $9x$.

Adding or subtracting terms forms an expression. In $2x^2 + 3x + 8$, the
three terms are $2x^2$, $3x$, and $8$.

**Example.** Identify the terms in each expression: (a) $9x^2 + 7x + 12$
(b) $8x + 3y$.

(a) The terms are $9x^2$, $7x$, and $12$. (b) The terms are $8x$ and $3y$.

If there are like terms in an expression, we can simplify by combining
them. What would $4x + 7x + x$ simplify to? Thinking of it as $4$ of
something, plus $7$ more, plus $1$ more, gives $12$ of that thing — add the
coefficients and keep the same variable, regardless of what $x$
represents: $4x + 7x + x = 12x$.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Combine like terms.**

  1. Identify like terms.
  2. Rearrange the expression so the like terms are together.
  3. Add or subtract the coefficients and keep the same variable for each
     group of like terms.
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** Simplify $2x^2 + 3x + 7 + x^2 + 4x + 5$.

Identify the like terms — $2x^2$ and $x^2$; $3x$ and $4x$; $7$ and $5$.
Rearrange so like terms are together: $2x^2 + x^2 + 3x + 4x + 7 + 5$.
Combine: $3x^2 + 7x + 12$.

## Translate an English Phrase to an Algebraic Expression

Now we'll reverse the earlier process and translate English phrases into
algebraic expressions.

| Operation | Phrase | Expression |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Addition | $a$ plus $b$; the sum of $a$ and $b$; $a$ increased by $b$; $b$ more than $a$; the total of $a$ and $b$; $b$ added to $a$ | $a + b$ |
| Subtraction | $a$ minus $b$; the difference of $a$ and $b$; $a$ decreased by $b$; $b$ less than $a$; $b$ subtracted from $a$ | $a - b$ |
| Multiplication | $a$ times $b$; the product of $a$ and $b$; twice $a$ | $a \cdot b,\ ab,\ a(b),\ (a)(b),\ 2a$ |
| Division | $a$ divided by $b$; the quotient of $a$ and $b$; the ratio of $a$ and $b$; $b$ divided into $a$ | $a \div b,\ a/b,\ \tfrac{a}{b},\ b)\overline{a}$ |

Each phrase involving two numbers tells us to operate on them, and the
words "of" and "and" mark where the numbers are.

**Example.** Translate each phrase into an algebraic expression: (a) the
difference of $17x$ and $5$ (b) the quotient of $10x^2$ and $7$.

(a) *Difference* means subtraction; look for "of" and "and" to find the
numbers to subtract: $17x - 5$.

(b) *Quotient* means division: $10x^2 \div 7$, also written
$10x^2/7$ or $\tfrac{10x^2}{7}$.

How old will you be in eight years? What age is $8$ more than your age
now? "Eight more than" means $8$ added to your present age. How old were
you seven years ago? "Seven less than" means $7$ subtracted from your
present age.

**Example.** Translate into an algebraic expression: (a) seventeen more
than $y$ (b) nine less than $9x^2$.

(a) *More than* means addition, and "more than" means "added to":
seventeen more than $y$ is seventeen added to $y$, so $y + 17$.

(b) *Less than* means subtraction, and "less than" means "subtracted
from": nine less than $9x^2$ is nine subtracted from $9x^2$, so
$9x^2 - 9$.

**Example.** Translate into an algebraic expression: (a) five times the
sum of $m$ and $n$ (b) the sum of five times $m$ and $n$.

(a) *Times* means multiply, *sum* means add. Because we're multiplying $5$
by the whole sum, we need parentheses around $m + n$ to force the sum
first (order of operations): $5(m+n)$.

(b) Here we're taking the sum of "five times $m$" and $n$ — look for "of"
and "and" to see what's being added: $5m + n$. Notice how the parentheses
in (a) change the meaning.

  answer="x + 14"
  hint="'More than' means addition — the number is added to $x$."
>}}

{{< fillin
  question="Translate into an algebraic expression: eight times the sum of $p$ and $q$."

  hint="Multiplying by the whole sum means the sum needs parentheses around it first."
>}}

Later in this course, we'll apply algebra to solving applications, and the
first step is translating an English phrase into an algebraic expression.

**Example.** The length of a rectangle is $6$ less than the width. Let $w$
represent the width. Write an expression for the length.

A phrase about the length: "$6$ less than the width." Substitute $w$ for
"the width": "$6$ less than $w$." Rewrite "less than" as "subtracted
from": "$6$ subtracted from $w$." Translate: $w - 6$.

**Example.** June has dimes and quarters in her purse. The number of dimes
is three less than four times the number of quarters. Let $q$ represent
the number of quarters. Write an expression for the number of dimes.

A phrase about the number of dimes: "three less than four times the
number of quarters." Substitute $q$ for "the number of quarters": "three
less than $4$ times $q$." Translate "$4$ times $q$" as $4q$: "three less
than $4q$." Translate the phrase into algebra: $4q - 3$.

## Key terms

**variable** — a letter that represents a number whose value may change.
**constant** — a number whose value always stays the same. **expression**
— a number, a variable, or a combination of numbers and variables using
operation symbols. **equation** — two expressions connected by an equal
sign. **base** and **exponent** — in $a^n$, the base $a$ is the number
being repeatedly multiplied, and the exponent $n$ tells how many times.
**term** — a constant, or the product of a constant and one or more
variables. **coefficient** — the constant that multiplies the variable in
a term. **like terms** — terms that are either constants or have the same
variables raised to the same powers.
