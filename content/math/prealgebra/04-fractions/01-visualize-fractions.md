## Understand the meaning of fractions

Andy and Bobby love pizza. On Monday night, they share a pizza equally. How
much of the pizza does each one get? There is one whole pizza, evenly
divided into two equal parts, so each boy gets one of the two equal parts.
In math, we write $\tfrac{1}{2}$ to mean one out of two parts.

On Tuesday, Andy and Bobby share a pizza with their parents, with each
person getting an equal amount of the whole pizza. There is one whole
pizza, divided evenly into four equal parts, so each person has
$\tfrac{1}{4}$ of the pizza. On Wednesday, the family invites some friends
over for a pizza dinner — twelve people in all. If they share the pizza
equally, each person gets $\tfrac{1}{12}$ of the pizza.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Fractions.** A fraction is written $\tfrac{a}{b}$, where $a$ and $b$
  are integers and $b \neq 0$. In a fraction, $a$ is called the
  **numerator** and $b$ is called the **denominator**.
{{< /callout >}}

A fraction is a way to represent parts of a whole. The denominator $b$
represents the number of equal parts the whole has been divided into, and
the numerator $a$ represents how many of those parts are included. The
denominator $b$ cannot equal zero because division by zero is undefined.

The circle above has been divided into three parts of equal size. Each
part represents $\tfrac{1}{3}$ of the circle. This kind of model is called
a **fraction circle** — other shapes, such as rectangles, can also model
fractions.

**Example.** Name the fraction of the shape that is shaded in each figure:
(a) a circle cut into eight equal wedges, five of them shaded; (b) a
$3 \times 3$ grid of nine equal squares, two of them shaded.

We ask two questions: how many equal parts are there (the denominator),
and of those, how many are shaded (the numerator)?

(a) There are eight equal parts, and five are shaded, so the fraction
shaded is $\tfrac{5}{8}$.

(b) There are nine equal parts, and two are shaded, so the fraction shaded
is $\tfrac{2}{9}$.

## Model improper fractions and mixed numbers

Suppose you had eight equal fifth-pieces of a pizza. You used five of them
to make one whole pizza, and you had three fifths left over. Using
fraction notation, eight fifths is written $\tfrac{8}{5}$. Since eight
fifths is one whole (five fifths), plus three more fifths, we can write it
as $1\tfrac{3}{5}$, read as "one and three-fifths."

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Mixed numbers.** A mixed number consists of a whole number $a$ and a
  fraction $\tfrac{b}{c}$ where $c \neq 0$. It is written
  $a\,\tfrac{b}{c}$.
{{< /callout >}}

Fractions such as $\tfrac{5}{4}$, $\tfrac{3}{2}$, $\tfrac{5}{5}$, and
$\tfrac{7}{3}$ are called **improper fractions** — the numerator is greater
than or equal to the denominator, so the fraction's value is greater than
or equal to one. When the numerator is smaller than the denominator, the
fraction is a **proper fraction**, with value less than one. Fractions
such as $\tfrac{1}{2}$, $\tfrac{3}{7}$, and $\tfrac{11}{18}$ are proper
fractions.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Proper and improper fractions.** The fraction $\tfrac{a}{b}$ is a
  **proper fraction** if $a < b$, and an **improper fraction** if
  $a \geq b$.
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** Name the improper fraction modeled by two circles, each cut
into thirds, with all three thirds of the first circle shaded and one of
the three thirds of the second circle shaded. Then write it as a mixed
number.

Each circle is divided into three pieces, so each piece is $\tfrac{1}{3}$
of a circle. There are four shaded pieces total, so there are four
thirds, $\tfrac{4}{3}$. The figure also shows one whole circle plus one
third, which is $1\tfrac{1}{3}$. So $\tfrac{4}{3} = 1\tfrac{1}{3}$.

## Convert between improper fractions and mixed numbers

The division expression $\tfrac{11}{6}$ (which can also be written as
$6 \overline{)11}$) tells us to find how many groups of $6$ are in $11$.
To convert an improper fraction to a mixed number without fraction
circles, we divide.

**Example.** Convert $\tfrac{33}{8}$ to a mixed number.

Divide the denominator into the numerator: $\tfrac{33}{8}$ means
$8 \overline{)33}$.

$$\begin{array}{r} 4 \\ 8\,\overline{\smash{)}\,33} \\ 32 \\ \hline 1 \end{array}$$

The quotient is $4$, the remainder is $1$, and the divisor is $8$. Write
the mixed number as quotient, plus remainder over divisor:

$$\frac{33}{8} = 4\frac{1}{8}$$

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Convert an improper fraction to a mixed number.**

  1. Divide the denominator into the numerator.
  2. Identify the quotient, remainder, and divisor.
  3. Write the mixed number as
     $\text{quotient} \; \tfrac{\text{remainder}}{\text{divisor}}$.
{{< /callout >}}

  answer="3\frac{2}{7}"
  answerDisplay="$3\tfrac{2}{7}$"
  hint="Divide $7$ into $23$. The quotient is the whole number, and the remainder goes over the divisor."
>}}

{{< fillin

  answer="4\frac{4}{11}"

To convert a mixed number to an improper fraction, we reverse the idea:
multiply the whole number by the denominator, add the numerator, and
write the result over the original denominator.

**Example.** Convert the mixed number $4\tfrac{2}{3}$ to an improper
fraction.

Multiply the whole number by the denominator: the whole number is $4$ and
the denominator is $3$, so $4 \cdot 3 = 12$. Add the numerator to the
product: $12 + 2 = 14$. Write the sum over the original denominator:

$$4\frac{2}{3} = \frac{14}{3}$$

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Convert a mixed number to an improper fraction.**

  1. Multiply the whole number by the denominator.
  2. Add the numerator to the product found in Step 1.
  3. Write the final sum over the original denominator.
{{< /callout >}}

## Model equivalent fractions

If Andy eats $\tfrac{1}{2}$ of a pizza and Bobby eats $\tfrac{2}{4}$ of an
identical pizza, have they eaten the same amount? In other words, does
$\tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{2}{4}$?

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Equivalent fractions.** Equivalent fractions are fractions that have
  the same value.
{{< /callout >}}

Imagine a rectangular tile representing one whole. A second identical
tile is cut into two equal halves, and a third identical tile is cut into
four equal fourths. Two of the fourth-pieces line up exactly with one
half-piece, so $\tfrac{2}{4} = \tfrac{1}{2}$. Likewise, three sixth-pieces
line up exactly with one half-piece, so $\tfrac{3}{6} = \tfrac{1}{2}$ as
well.

  question="How many eighths equal one-fourth? Answer as a fraction with denominator 8."
  answer="\frac{2}{8}"
  answerDisplay="$\tfrac{2}{8}$"
  hint="Think of a tile cut into fourths lined up against an identical tile cut into eighths."
>}}

## Find equivalent fractions

How can we use mathematics, rather than pictures, to change $\tfrac{1}{2}$
into $\tfrac{4}{8}$? Imagine cutting a pizza that's divided into two
pieces, and cutting each of those two pieces into four smaller pieces.
The whole pizza is now cut into eight pieces instead of two. Mathematically:

$$\frac{1 \cdot 4}{2 \cdot 4} = \frac{4}{8}$$

This leads to the **Equivalent Fractions Property**: multiplying the
numerator and denominator of a fraction by the same nonzero number does
not change the fraction's value.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Equivalent Fractions Property.** If $a$, $b$, and $c$ are numbers
  where $b \neq 0$ and $c \neq 0$, then

  $$\frac{a}{b} = \frac{a \cdot c}{b \cdot c}$$
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** Find three fractions equivalent to $\tfrac{2}{5}$.

To find equivalent fractions, we multiply the numerator and denominator
by the same number (but not zero). Multiplying by $2$, $3$, and $5$:

$$\frac{2 \cdot 2}{5 \cdot 2} = \frac{4}{10}, \qquad \frac{2 \cdot 3}{5 \cdot 3} = \frac{6}{15}, \qquad \frac{2 \cdot 5}{5 \cdot 5} = \frac{10}{25}$$

So $\tfrac{4}{10}$, $\tfrac{6}{15}$, and $\tfrac{10}{25}$ are all
equivalent to $\tfrac{2}{5}$.

{{< fillin
  question="Find a fraction equivalent to $\tfrac{3}{5}$ by multiplying the numerator and denominator by 4."
  answer="\frac{12}{20}"
  answerDisplay="$\tfrac{12}{20}$"
  hint="Multiply both the numerator $3$ and the denominator $5$ by $4$."
>}}

**Example.** Find a fraction with a denominator of $21$ that is
equivalent to $\tfrac{2}{7}$.

We need to multiply the denominator by a number that results in $21$.
Since $7 \cdot 3 = 21$, we multiply both the numerator and denominator by
$3$:

$$\frac{2}{7} = \frac{2 \cdot 3}{7 \cdot 3} = \frac{6}{21}$$

## Locate fractions and mixed numbers on the number line

Now we are ready to plot fractions on a number line. This helps visualize
fractions and understand their values.

To locate a proper fraction like $\tfrac{1}{5}$, we know its value is less
than one, so it lies between the whole numbers $0$ and $1$. We divide the
segment of the number line between $0$ and $1$ into five equal parts, and
plot $\tfrac{1}{5}$ at the first mark.

To locate a mixed number like $3\tfrac{1}{3}$, we first note that a mixed
number is a whole number plus a proper fraction, so $3\tfrac{1}{3} > 3$
but not a whole unit greater — it lies between $3$ and $4$. We divide the
number line between $3$ and $4$ into three equal parts (thirds) and plot
$3\tfrac{1}{3}$ at the first mark.

To locate an improper fraction like $\tfrac{7}{4}$, it is easier to first
convert it to a mixed number: $\tfrac{7}{4} = 1\tfrac{3}{4}$. This tells
us it lies between $1$ and $2$, one-fourth of the way past the first
mark... actually three-fourths of the way. We divide the segment between
$1$ and $2$ into four equal parts and plot the point at the third mark.

**Example.** Locate and label $\tfrac{3}{4}$, $\tfrac{4}{3}$,
$\tfrac{5}{3}$, $4\tfrac{1}{5}$, and $\tfrac{7}{2}$ on a number line.

Start with the proper fraction $\tfrac{3}{4}$: it's between $0$ and $1$.
Divide that segment into four equal parts and plot $\tfrac{3}{4}$ at the
third mark.

Next, locate the mixed number $4\tfrac{1}{5}$: it's between $4$ and $5$.
Divide that segment into five equal parts, and plot the point one-fifth
of the way from $4$.

Now locate the improper fractions $\tfrac{4}{3}$ and $\tfrac{5}{3}$. It's
easier to convert them to mixed numbers first:
$\tfrac{4}{3} = 1\tfrac{1}{3}$ and $\tfrac{5}{3} = 1\tfrac{2}{3}$. Divide
the distance between $1$ and $2$ into thirds, and plot both points.

Finally, plot $\tfrac{7}{2}$. Written as a mixed number,
$\tfrac{7}{2} = 3\tfrac{1}{2}$, so it lies exactly halfway between $3$ and
$4$.

Thinking of negative fractions as the opposite of positive fractions
helps locate them on the number line, the same way we located the
opposites of whole numbers in the integers chapter. To locate
$-\tfrac{15}{8}$, first find $\tfrac{15}{8}$: as a mixed number it's
$1\tfrac{7}{8}$, so it lies between $1$ and $2$. Its opposite,
$-\tfrac{15}{8}$, is the same distance from $0$ but on the other side, so
it lies between $-1$ and $-2$.

## Order fractions and mixed numbers

We can use inequality symbols to order fractions. Remember that $a > b$
means $a$ is to the right of $b$ on the number line; as we move left to
right on a number line, the values increase.

**Example.** Order each pair using $<$ or $>$: (a) $-\tfrac{2}{3}$ and
$-1$; (b) $-3\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $-3$; (c) $-\tfrac{3}{7}$ and
$-\tfrac{3}{8}$; (d) $-2$ and $\tfrac{-16}{9}$.

(a) Plotting both on a number line, $-\tfrac{2}{3}$ is to the right of
$-1$, so $-\tfrac{2}{3} > -1$.

(b) $-3\tfrac{1}{2}$ is farther left (more negative) than $-3$, so
$-3\tfrac{1}{2} < -3$.

(c) Both are negative proper fractions; converting to a common denominator
of $56$ shows $-\tfrac{3}{7} = -\tfrac{24}{56}$ is to the right of
$-\tfrac{3}{8} = -\tfrac{21}{56}$... in fact $-\tfrac{24}{56}$ is farther
left, so $-\tfrac{3}{7} < -\tfrac{3}{8}$.

(d) $\tfrac{-16}{9}$ written as a mixed number is $-1\tfrac{7}{9}$, which
is to the right of $-2$, so $-2 < \tfrac{-16}{9}$.

## Key terms

**fraction** — a number written $\tfrac{a}{b}$ with $a$ the numerator and
$b \neq 0$ the denominator, representing parts of a whole. **proper
fraction** — a fraction whose numerator is smaller than its denominator
(value less than one). **improper fraction** — a fraction whose numerator
is greater than or equal to its denominator (value at least one). **mixed
number** — a whole number combined with a proper fraction. **equivalent
fractions** — fractions that represent the same value. **Equivalent
Fractions Property** — multiplying (or dividing) the numerator and
denominator of a fraction by the same nonzero number leaves its value
unchanged.
