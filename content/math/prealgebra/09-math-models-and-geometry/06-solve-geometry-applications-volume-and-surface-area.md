## Find volume and surface area of rectangular solids

The amount of paint needed to cover the outside of a box is its **surface
area** — a square measure of the total area of all the sides. The amount
of space inside the box is its **volume** — a cubic measure.

Each box is in the shape of a **rectangular solid**, with dimensions
length, width, and height. Consider a rectangular solid with length $4$
units, width $2$ units, and height $3$ units. Breaking it into layers makes
it easy to see: the top layer has $8$ cubic units, the middle layer has
$8$ cubic units, and the bottom layer has $8$ cubic units, for $24$ cubic
units altogether. Notice that $24$ is the length times the width times the
height.

$$V = L \cdot W \cdot H \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad 24 = 4 \cdot 2 \cdot 3$$

The volume, $V$, of any rectangular solid is the product of the length,
width, and height: $V = LWH$. We can also write the formula in terms of the
area of the base: the area of the base, $B$, is $L \cdot W$, so
substituting gives $V = Bh$.

To find the surface area, think about finding the area of each face. A
rectangular solid has $6$ faces, and for each face you see there is an
identical opposite face that doesn't show, so:

$$S = 2LH + 2LW + 2WH$$

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Volume and surface area of a rectangular solid.** For a rectangular
  solid with length $L$, width $W$, and height $H$:

  $$
  \text{Volume: } V = LWH \qquad \text{Surface Area: }
  S = 2LH + 2LW + 2WH
  $$
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** For a rectangular solid with length $14$ cm, height $17$ cm,
and width $9$ cm, find (a) the volume and (b) the surface area.

(a) Let $V =$ volume. Substituting into $V = LWH$:

$$V = 14 \cdot 9 \cdot 17 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad V = 2{,}142$$

The volume is $2{,}142$ cubic centimeters.

(b) Let $S =$ surface area. Substituting into $S = 2LH + 2LW + 2WH$:

$$
S = 2(14 \cdot 17) + 2(14 \cdot 9) + 2(9 \cdot 17)
\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad S = 1{,}034
$$

The surface area is $1{,}034$ square centimeters.

### Volume and surface area of a cube

A **cube** is a rectangular solid whose length, width, and height are all
equal. Substituting $s$ for the length, width, and height into the
rectangular solid formulas gives $V = s^3$ and $S = 6s^2$.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Volume and surface area of a cube.** For any cube with sides of
  length $s$:

  $$\text{Volume: } V = s^3 \qquad \text{Surface Area: } S = 6s^2$$
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** A cube is $2.5$ inches on each side. Find its (a) volume and
(b) surface area.

(a) $V = s^3 = (2.5)^3 = 15.625$. The volume is $15.625$ cubic inches.

(b) $S = 6s^2 = 6 \cdot (2.5)^2 = 37.5$. The surface area is $37.5$
square inches.

**Example.** A notepad cube measures $2$ inches on each side. Find its

(a) $V = 2^3 = 8$. The volume is $8$ cubic inches.

## Find the volume and surface area of spheres

A **sphere** is the shape of a basketball, like a three-dimensional
circle. Just like a circle, the size of a sphere is determined by its
radius, the distance from the center of the sphere to any point on its
surface. We'll approximate $\pi$ with $3.14$.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Volume and surface area of a sphere.** For a sphere with radius $r$:

  $$
  \text{Volume: } V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \qquad \text{Surface Area: }
  S = 4\pi r^2
  $$
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** A sphere has a radius $6$ inches. Find its (a) volume and
(b) surface area.

(a) $V = \tfrac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \approx \tfrac{4}{3}(3.14)6^3 \approx 904.32$.
The volume is approximately $904.32$ cubic inches.

(b) $S = 4\pi r^2 \approx 4(3.14)6^2 \approx 452.16$. The surface area is
approximately $452.16$ square inches.

## Find the volume and surface area of a cylinder

A **cylinder** is a solid figure with two parallel circles of the same
size at the top and bottom, called the bases. The height $h$ of a cylinder
is the distance between the two bases; for the cylinders here, the sides
and height are perpendicular to the bases.

Rectangular solids and cylinders are similar because they both have two
bases and a height, so the formula for volume of a rectangular solid,
$V = Bh$, can also be used for a cylinder — but now the base area, $B$, is
the area of a circle, $\pi r^2$:

$$V = Bh = (\pi r^2) \times h = \pi r^2 h$$

To understand the surface area formula, think of a can of vegetables. It
has three surfaces: the top, the bottom, and the piece that forms the
sides. If you carefully cut the label off the side and unroll it, you'll
see that it is a rectangle. The distance around the edge of the can is the
circumference of the cylinder's base, and this is also the length $L$ of
the rectangular label; the height of the cylinder is the width $W$ of the
label. So the area of the label is $A = L \cdot W = 2\pi r \cdot h$. Adding
the areas of the two circles to the area of the rectangle:

$$
S = A_{\text{top circle}} + A_{\text{bottom circle}} +
A_{\text{rectangle}} = \pi r^2 + \pi r^2 + 2\pi r \cdot h = 2\pi r^2 +
2\pi rh
$$

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Volume and surface area of a cylinder.** For a cylinder with radius
  $r$ and height $h$:

  $$
  \text{Volume: } V = \pi r^2 h \text{ (or } V = Bh\text{)} \qquad
  \text{Surface Area: } S = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi rh
  $$
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** A cylinder has height $5$ inches and radius $3$ inches. Find
its (a) volume and (b) surface area.

(a) $V = \pi r^2 h \approx (3.14)3^2 \cdot 5 \approx 141.3$. The volume is
approximately $141.3$ cubic inches.

(b) $S = 2\pi r^2 + 2\pi rh \approx 2(3.14)3^2 + 2(3.14)(3)5 \approx
150.72$. The surface area is approximately $150.72$ square inches.

## Find the volume of cones

The first image many of us have when we hear the word "cone" is an ice
cream cone. In geometry, a **cone** is a solid figure with one circular
base and a vertex. The height of a cone is the distance between its base
and the vertex; the cones here always have the height perpendicular to
the base.

We saw that the volume of a cylinder is $V = \pi r^2 h$. If we picture a
cone placed inside a cylinder with the same height and same base, the
volume of the cone is less than that of the cylinder — in fact, the volume
of a cone is exactly one-third of the volume of a cylinder with the same
base and height:

$$V = \tfrac{1}{3}Bh$$

Since the base of a cone is a circle, we substitute $\pi r^2$ for $B$:

$$V = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$$

We only find the volume of a cone in this book, not its surface area.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Volume of a cone.** For a cone with radius $r$ and height $h$:

  $$V = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$$
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** Find the volume of a cone with height $6$ inches and radius
of its base $2$ inches.

$$
V = \tfrac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h \approx \tfrac{1}{3}(3.14)(2)^2(6) \approx
25.12
$$

The volume is approximately $25.12$ cubic inches.

$$V \approx \tfrac{1}{3}(3.14)(2.5)^2(8) \approx 52.33$$

The volume of the wrap is approximately $52.33$ cubic inches.

## Key terms

**rectangular solid** — a three-dimensional figure with six rectangular
faces (length, width, height). **surface area** — a square measure of the
total area of all the faces of a solid. **volume** — a cubic measure of
the space enclosed by a solid. **cube** — a rectangular solid whose
length, width, and height are all equal. **sphere** — a three-dimensional
figure where every point on the surface is the same distance (the radius)
from the center. **cylinder** — a solid figure with two parallel congruent
circular bases connected by a curved surface perpendicular to the bases.
**cone** — a solid figure with one circular base tapering to a single
vertex.
