## Use division notation

So far we have explored addition, subtraction, and multiplication. Now let's
consider division. Suppose you have $12$ cookies and want to package them in
bags with $4$ cookies in each bag. How many bags would you need? You might
put $4$ cookies in the first bag, $4$ in the second, and so on until you run
out — filling $3$ bags. In other words, starting with $12$ cookies, you
subtract $4$ at a time. Division is a way to represent repeated subtraction,
just as multiplication represents repeated addition. Instead of subtracting
$4$ repeatedly, we write

$$12 \div 4$$

We read this as *twelve divided by four*, and the result is the **quotient**
of $12$ and $4$. The quotient is $3$ because we can subtract $4$ from $12$
exactly $3$ times. The number being divided is the **dividend**, and the
number dividing it is the **divisor**. Here $12$ is the dividend and $4$ is
the divisor.

Division can be written several ways — with the division sign, as a
fraction, with a slash, or with the long division bracket:

$$12 \div 4 \qquad \frac{12}{4} \qquad 12/4 \qquad 4\overline{)12}$$

In each case, $12$ is the dividend and $4$ is the divisor. Division is
performed on two numbers at a time; when translating between notation and
words, look for the words *of* and *and* to identify the numbers.

**Example.** Translate from math notation to words: (a) $64 \div 8$
(b) $\frac{42}{7}$ (c) $4\overline{)28}$.

(a) *Sixty-four divided by eight*; the result is the quotient of sixty-four
and eight. (b) *Forty-two divided by seven*; the result is the quotient of
forty-two and seven. (c) *Twenty-eight divided by four*; the result is the
quotient of twenty-eight and four.

## Model division of whole numbers

As with multiplication, we model division using counters — here, division
organizes items into equal groups. To find the quotient $24 \div 8$, we want
to know how many groups of $8$ are in $24$. Model the dividend by starting
with $24$ counters, then form groups of $8$ counters — the divisor tells us
how many counters go in each group. Counting the groups, there are $3$:

$$24 \div 8 = 3$$

## Divide whole numbers

We said that addition and subtraction are inverse operations because one
undoes the other. Similarly, division is the inverse operation of
multiplication. We know $12 \div 4 = 3$ because $3 \cdot 4 = 12$. Knowing
the multiplication facts is very important when doing division — and we
check a division by multiplying the quotient by the divisor to see if it
equals the dividend. We know $24 \div 8 = 3$ is correct because
$3 \cdot 8 = 24$.

For example, $42 \div 6 = 7$ (check: $7 \cdot 6 = 42$ ✓), $\frac{72}{9} = 8$
(check: $8 \cdot 9 = 72$ ✓), and $63 \div 7 = 9$ (check: $9 \cdot 7 = 63$ ✓).

What is the quotient when you divide a number by itself?

$$\frac{15}{15} = 1 \quad \text{because} \quad 1 \cdot 15 = 15$$

**Division Properties of One:** any number (except $0$) divided by itself is
one, $a \div a = 1$; and any number divided by one is the same number,
$a \div 1 = a$. So $11 \div 11 = 1$, $\frac{19}{1} = 19$, and
$1\overline{)7}$ gives $7$.

What about dividing with zero? Suppose we have \$0 and want to divide it
among $3$ people — each person would get \$0. Zero divided by any number is
$0$. But now suppose we want to divide \$10 by $0$: we would need a number
that we multiply by $0$ to get $10$, and that cannot happen, because $0$
times any number is $0$.

**Division Properties of Zero:** zero divided by any number is zero,
$0 \div a = 0$; dividing a number by zero is **undefined**, $a \div 0$ has
no answer. Another way to see this: division is repeated subtraction, and
subtracting $0$ from $10$ will never change the total, so we never get an
answer.

When the divisor or the dividend has more than one digit, it is usually
easier to use the bracket notation — a process called **long division**.
Let's divide $78$ by $3$. The divisor $3$ goes into $7$ (the first digit of
the dividend) two times, since $2 \times 3 = 6$: write the $2$ above the
$7$, write the product $6$ under the $7$, and subtract, leaving $1$. Bring
down the next digit, $8$, making $18$; the divisor $3$ goes into $18$ six
times, so write $6$ in the quotient above the $8$. Multiply $6 \times 3 =
18$ and subtract — nothing remains, and there are no more digits to bring
down, so the division is finished:

$$78 \div 3 = 26$$

Check by multiplying the quotient times the divisor: $26 \times 3 = 78$. ✓

To divide whole numbers:

1. Divide the first digit of the dividend by the divisor. If the divisor is
   larger than the first digit of the dividend, divide the first two digits
   of the dividend by the divisor, and so on.
2. Write the quotient above the dividend.
3. Multiply the quotient by the divisor and write the product under the
   dividend.
4. Subtract that product from the dividend.
5. Bring down the next digit of the dividend.
6. Repeat from step 1 until there are no more digits in the dividend to
   bring down.
7. Check by multiplying the quotient times the divisor.

**Example.** Divide $2{,}596 \div 4$. Since $4$ does not go into $2$, use
the first two digits: $4$ goes into $25$ six times. Write the $6$, multiply
$6 \cdot 4 = 24$, subtract to leave $1$, and bring down the $9$. There are
$4$ fours in $19$; write the $4$, subtract $16$ from $19$ to leave $3$, and
bring down the $6$. There are $9$ fours in $36$, with nothing left over. So
$2{,}596 \div 4 = 649$. Check: $649 \times 4 = 2{,}596$. ✓

**Example.** Divide $4{,}506 \div 6$. $6$ doesn't go into $4$, so start with
$45$: there are $7$ sixes in $45$, with $3$ left over. Bring down the $0$,
making $30$; there are $5$ sixes in $30$, exactly. Bring down the $6$; there
is $1$ six in $6$, exactly. So $4{,}506 \div 6 = 751$. Check:
$751 \times 6 = 4{,}506$. ✓

**Example.** Divide $7{,}263 \div 9$. Since $9$ doesn't go into $7$, start
with $72$: there are $8$ nines in $72$, exactly. Bring down the $6$; there
are $0$ nines in $6$, so write a $0$ in the quotient. Bring down the $3$,
making $63$; there are $7$ nines in $63$. So $7{,}263 \div 9 = 807$. Check:
$807 \times 9 = 7{,}263$. ✓ (Keep an eye out for zeros in the quotient —
they hold their place just like any other digit.)

So far all our divisions have worked out evenly, but that isn't always so.
Suppose there were $28$ cookies to put in bags of $8$: there would be $3$
groups of eight, with $4$ cookies left over. We call the $4$ the
**remainder**, and show it by writing R4 next to the $3$ — the quotient is
$3$ R$4$. To check a division with a remainder, multiply the quotient by the
divisor, then *add the remainder*: $3 \times 8 = 24$, and $24 + 4 = 28$. ✓

**Example.** Divide $1{,}439 \div 4$. Working through the long division:
$4$ into $14$ goes $3$ times leaving $2$; bring down the $3$ to make $23$,
which holds $5$ fours, leaving $3$; bring down the $9$ to make $39$, which
holds $9$ fours, leaving $3$. There are no more digits to bring down, so
$1{,}439 \div 4$ is $359$ with a remainder of $3$. Check:
$359 \cdot 4 = 1{,}436$, and $1{,}436 + 3 = 1{,}439$. ✓

Divisors can have more than one digit too. Dividing $1{,}461 \div 13$ the
same way gives $112$ R$5$ (check: $112 \cdot 13 = 1{,}456$, plus $5$ is
$1{,}461$ ✓). Dividing $74{,}521 \div 241$ gives $309$ R$52$. Sometimes it
might not be obvious how many times the divisor goes into digits of the
dividend — we have to guess and check to find the greatest number that goes
in without exceeding them.

## Translate word phrases to math notation

Some of the words that indicate division:

| Word phrase    | Example                        | Expression                                            |
| :------------- | :----------------------------- | :---------------------------------------------------- |
| divided by     | $12$ divided by $4$            | $12 \div 4$, $\frac{12}{4}$, $12/4$, $4\overline{)12}$ |
| quotient of    | the quotient of $12$ and $4$   | as above                                              |
| divided into   | $4$ divided into $12$          | as above                                              |

**Example.** Translate and simplify: the quotient of $51$ and $17$. The word
*quotient* tells us to divide: $51 \div 17 = 3$. We could just as correctly
have written it $17\overline{)51}$ or $\frac{51}{17}$.

## Divide whole numbers in applications

We use the same strategy as in previous sections: determine what we are
looking for, write a phrase, translate it to math notation, simplify, and
answer with a complete sentence.

**Example.** Cecelia bought a $160$-ounce box of oatmeal at the big box
store. She wants to divide it into $8$-ounce servings, one bag for each work
day. How many servings will she get from the big box? The phrase is *$160$
ounces divided by $8$ ounces*, which translates to $160 \div 8 = 20$.
Cecelia will get $20$ servings from the big box.

## Key terms

**quotient** — the result of dividing one number by another. **dividend** —
the number being divided. **divisor** — the number dividing the dividend.
**remainder** — the amount left over when a division does not come out
evenly. **undefined** — having no answer; dividing a number by zero is
undefined. **long division** — the digit-by-digit process of dividing using
the bracket notation.
