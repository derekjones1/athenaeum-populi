## Simplify expressions using the distributive property

Suppose three friends are going to the movies. They each need \$9.25 —
that's $9$ dollars and $1$ quarter. How much money do they need altogether?
You can think about the dollars separately from the quarters: $3$ times
\$9 is \$27, and $3$ times $1$ quarter is $75$ cents. In total, they need
\$27.75.

If you think about doing the math this way, you are using the
**Distributive Property**.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Distributive Property.** If $a$, $b$, $c$ are real numbers, then

  $$a(b + c) = ab + ac$$
{{< /callout >}}

Back to our friends at the movies, we could show the math steps we take to
find the total amount of money they need like this:

$$3(9.25) = 3(9 + 0.25) = 3(9) + 3(0.25) = 27 + 0.75 = 27.75$$

In algebra, we use the Distributive Property to remove parentheses as we
simplify expressions. For example, if we are asked to simplify the
expression $3(x + 4)$, the order of operations says to work in the
parentheses first. But we cannot add $x$ and $4$, since they are not like
terms. So we use the Distributive Property, as shown in the next example.

**Example.** Simplify: $3(x + 4)$.

Distribute, then multiply:

$$3(x + 4) = 3 \cdot x + 3 \cdot 4 = 3x + 12$$

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $4(x + 2)$."
  answer="4x + 8"
  hint="Distribute the $4$ to both $x$ and $2$, then multiply each term."
>}}

Some students find it helpful to draw arrows to remind them how to use the
Distributive Property — an arrow from the $6$ to each term inside the
parentheses, so $6(5y + 1)$ becomes $6 \cdot 5y + 6 \cdot 1 = 30y + 6$.

The distributive property can be used to simplify expressions that look
slightly different from $a(b + c)$. Here are two other forms:

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Distributive Property — other forms.** If $a$, $b$, $c$ are real
  numbers, then

  $$a(b - c) = ab - ac \qquad\qquad (b + c)a = ba + ca$$
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** Simplify: $2(x - 3)$.

Distribute, then multiply:

$$2(x - 3) = 2 \cdot x - 2 \cdot 3 = 2x - 6$$

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $7(x - 6)$."
  answer="7x - 42"
  hint="Distribute the $7$ to both $x$ and $6$, keeping the subtraction."
>}}

Do you remember how to multiply a fraction by a whole number? We'll need to
do that in the next two examples.

**Example.** Simplify: $\tfrac{3}{4}(n + 12)$.

Distribute, then simplify:

$$
\tfrac{3}{4}(n + 12) = \tfrac{3}{4} \cdot n + \tfrac{3}{4} \cdot 12 =
\tfrac{3}{4}n + 9
$$

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $\tfrac{2}{5}(p + 10)$."
  answer="\frac{2}{5}p + 4"
  answerDisplay="$\tfrac{2}{5}p + 4$"
  hint="Distribute $\tfrac{2}{5}$ to both $p$ and $10$; $\tfrac{2}{5}$ of $10$ is $4$."
>}}

**Example.** Simplify: $8\left(\tfrac{3}{8}x + \tfrac{1}{4}\right)$.

Distribute, then multiply:

$$8 \cdot \tfrac{3}{8}x + 8 \cdot \tfrac{1}{4} = 3x + 2$$

Using the Distributive Property as shown in the next example will be very
useful when we solve money applications later.

**Example.** Simplify: $100(0.3 + 0.25q)$.

Distribute, then multiply:

$$100(0.3) + 100(0.25q) = 30 + 25q$$

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $100(0.7 + 0.15p)$."
  answer="70 + 15p"
  hint="Distribute the $100$ to both $0.7$ and $0.15p$."
>}}

In the next example we'll multiply by a variable. We'll need to do this in
a later chapter.

**Example.** Simplify: $m(n - 4)$.

Distribute, then multiply:

$$m \cdot n - m \cdot 4 = mn - 4m$$

Notice that we wrote $m \cdot 4$ as $4m$. We can do this because of the
Commutative Property of Multiplication — when a term is the product of a
number and a variable, we write the number first.

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $r(s - 2)$."
  answer="rs - 2r"
  hint="Distribute $r$ to both $s$ and $2$, then write the number first in the second term ($2r$, not $r2$)."
>}}

The next example uses the "backwards" form of the Distributive Property,
$(b + c)a = ba + ca$.

**Example.** Simplify: $(x + 8)p$.

Distribute:

$$(x + 8)p = px + 8p$$

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $(y + 4)q$."
  answer="qy + 4q"
  hint="Distribute $q$ to both $y$ and $4$, writing the number first in the constant term."
>}}

When you distribute a negative number, you need to be extra careful to get
the signs correct.

**Example.** Simplify: $-2(4y + 1)$.

Distribute, then simplify:

$$-2 \cdot 4y + (-2) \cdot 1 = -8y - 2$$

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $-3(6m + 5)$."
  answer="-18m - 15"
  hint="Distribute $-3$ to both $6m$ and $5$, keeping careful track of the signs."
>}}

**Example.** Simplify: $-11(4 - 3a)$.

Distribute, then simplify:

$$-11 \cdot 4 - (-11) \cdot 3a = -44 - (-33a) = -44 + 33a$$

You could also write the result as $33a - 44$. Do you know why?

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $-5(2 - 3a)$."
  answer="15a - 10"
  hint="Distribute $-5$ to both $2$ and $3a$; subtracting a negative flips a sign."
>}}

In the next example, we will show how to use the Distributive Property to
find the opposite of an expression. Remember, $-a = -1 \cdot a$.

**Example.** Simplify: $-(y + 5)$.

Multiplying by $-1$ results in the opposite, then distribute:

$$-1(y + 5) = -1 \cdot y + (-1) \cdot 5 = -y + (-5) = -y - 5$$

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $-(z - 11)$."
  answer="-z + 11"
  hint="Rewrite as $-1(z - 11)$, then distribute $-1$ to both terms."
>}}

Sometimes we need to use the Distributive Property as part of the order of
operations. Start by looking at the parentheses — if the expression inside
cannot be simplified, the next step is to multiply using the Distributive
Property, which removes the parentheses.

**Example.** Simplify: $8 - 2(x + 3)$.

Distribute, multiply, then combine like terms:

$$8 - 2 \cdot x - 2 \cdot 3 = 8 - 2x - 6 = -2x + 2$$

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $9 - 3(x + 2)$."
  answer="-3x + 3"
  hint="Distribute the $-3$ across $(x + 2)$ first, then combine the constant terms."
>}}

**Example.** Simplify: $4(x - 8) - (x + 3)$.

Distribute both terms, then combine like terms:

$$4x - 32 - x - 3 = 3x - 35$$

{{< fillin
  question="Simplify: $6(x - 9) - (x + 12)$."
  answer="5x - 66"
  hint="Distribute the $6$ across $(x - 9)$ and the implied $-1$ across $(x + 12)$, then combine like terms."
>}}

## Evaluate expressions using the distributive property

Some students need to be convinced that the Distributive Property always
works. In the examples below, we'll practice evaluating some of the
expressions from previous examples — in part (a), we'll evaluate the form
with parentheses, and in part (b) we'll evaluate the form we got after
distributing. If we evaluate both expressions correctly, this will show
that they are indeed equal.

**Example.** When $y = 10$, evaluate: (a) $6(5y + 1)$ (b) $6 \cdot 5y + 6
\cdot 1$.

(a) Substituting $10$ for $y$ and simplifying in the parentheses first:

$$6(5 \cdot 10 + 1) = 6(51) = 306$$

(b) Substituting $10$ for $y$ and simplifying:

$$6 \cdot 5 \cdot 10 + 6 \cdot 1 = 300 + 6 = 306$$

Notice that the answers are the same: when $y = 10$, $6(5y + 1) = 6 \cdot
5y + 6 \cdot 1$.

{{< fillin
  question="Evaluate $5(5w + 9)$ when $w = 3$."
  answer="120"
  hint="Simplify inside the parentheses first: $5(3) + 9 = 24$, then multiply by $5$."
>}}

**Example.** When $y = 3$, evaluate: (a) $-2(4y + 1)$ (b) $-2 \cdot 4y +
(-2) \cdot 1$.

Both forms evaluate to $-26$, confirming that $-2(4y + 1) = -8y - 2$ when
$y = 3$.

{{< fillin
  question="Evaluate $-6(8n + 11)$ when $n = -2$."
  answer="30"
  hint="Simplify inside the parentheses first: $8(-2) + 11 = -5$, then multiply by $-6$."
>}}

**Example.** When $y = 35$, evaluate (a) $-(y + 5)$ and (b) $-y - 5$, to
show that $-(y + 5) = -y - 5$.

(a) Substituting $35$ for $y$: $-(35 + 5) = -(40) = -40$.

(b) Substituting $35$ for $y$: $-35 - 5 = -40$.

The answers are the same when $y = 35$, demonstrating that $-(y + 5) = -y -
5$.

{{< fillin
  question="Evaluate $-(x - 4)$ when $x = 36$."
  answer="-32"
  hint="Simplify inside the parentheses first: $36 - 4 = 32$, then take the opposite."
>}}

## Key terms

**Distributive Property** — for real numbers $a$, $b$, $c$: $a(b + c) = ab +
ac$; also written $a(b - c) = ab - ac$ and $(b + c)a = ba + ca$. Multiplying
a sum (or difference) by a number gives the same result as multiplying each
term by that number and then adding (or subtracting) the products.
