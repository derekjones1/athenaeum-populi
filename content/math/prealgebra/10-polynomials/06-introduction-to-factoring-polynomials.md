## Find the greatest common factor of two or more expressions

Earlier we multiplied factors together to get a product. Now, we will be
reversing this process; we will start with a product and then break it down
into its factors. Splitting a product into factors is called **factoring**.

$$8 \cdot 7 = 56 \qquad\qquad 2x(x + 3) = 2x^2 + 6x$$

Reading the first equation from left to right, $8$ and $7$ are *factors* and
$56$ is the *product*. Reading it from right to left instead — starting from
the product and breaking it into factors — is factoring.

In an earlier chapter we factored numbers to find the least common multiple
(LCM) of two or more numbers. Now we will factor expressions and find the
*greatest common factor* of two or more expressions. The method we use is
similar to what we used to find the LCM.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Greatest common factor.** The greatest common factor (GCF) of two or
  more expressions is the largest expression that is a factor of all the
  expressions.
{{< /callout >}}

First we will find the greatest common factor of two numbers.

**Example.** Find the greatest common factor of $24$ and $36$.

Factor each number into primes, writing all variables with exponents in
expanded form:

$$24 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \qquad\qquad 36 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 3$$

List all the factors, matching common factors in a column, and circle the
factors that are shared by both numbers — here, two $2\text{s}$ and one
$3$:

$$24 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot \phantom{3 \cdot {}} 2 \cdot 3 \qquad\qquad 36 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 3$$

Bring down the common factors and multiply them:

$$\text{GCF} = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 = 12$$

The GCF of $24$ and $36$ is $12$. Notice that since the GCF is a factor of
both numbers, $24$ and $36$ can be written as multiples of $12$:

$$24 = 12 \cdot 2 \qquad\qquad 36 = 12 \cdot 3$$

In the previous example, we found the greatest common factor of constants.
The greatest common factor of an algebraic expression can contain variables
raised to powers along with coefficients. We summarize the steps we use to
find the greatest common factor.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Find the greatest common factor.**

  1. Factor each coefficient into primes. Write all variables with
     exponents in expanded form.
  2. List all factors — matching common factors in a column. In each
     column, circle the common factors.
  3. Bring down the common factors that all expressions share.
  4. Multiply the factors.
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** Find the greatest common factor of $5x$ and $15$.

Factor each number into primes, and circle the common factors in each
column:

$$5x = 5 \cdot x \qquad\qquad 15 = 3 \cdot 5$$

Bring down the common factor: $\text{GCF} = 5$. The GCF of $5x$ and $15$ is
$5$.

In the examples so far, the greatest common factor was a constant. In the
next two examples we will get variables in the greatest common factor.

**Example.** Find the greatest common factor of $12x^2$ and $18x^3$.

Factor each coefficient into primes and write the variables with exponents
in expanded form, then circle the common factors in each column:

$$12x^2 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot x \cdot x \qquad\qquad 18x^3 = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 3 \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x$$

Bring down the common factors and multiply:

$$\text{GCF} = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot x \cdot x = 6x^2$$

The GCF of $12x^2$ and $18x^3$ is $6x^2$.

**Example.** Find the greatest common factor of $14x^3$, $8x^2$, and $10x$.

Factor each coefficient into primes and write the variables with exponents
in expanded form, then circle the common factors:

$$14x^3 = 2 \cdot 7 \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x \qquad 8x^2 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 \cdot x \cdot x \qquad 10x = 2 \cdot 5 \cdot x$$

All three share one factor of $2$ and one factor of $x$:

$$\text{GCF} = 2 \cdot x = 2x$$

The GCF of $14x^3$, $8x^2$, and $10x$ is $2x$.

## Factor the greatest common factor from a polynomial

Just like in arithmetic, where it is sometimes useful to represent a number
in factored form (for example, $12$ as $2 \cdot 6$ or $3 \cdot 4$), in
algebra it can be useful to represent a polynomial in factored form. One way
to do this is by finding the greatest common factor of all the terms.
Remember that you can multiply a polynomial by a monomial as follows:

$$2(x + 7) = 2 \cdot x + 2 \cdot 7 = 2x + 14$$

Here, we will start with a product, like $2x + 14$, and end with its
factors, $2(x + 7)$. To do this we apply the Distributive Property "in
reverse."

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Distributive Property.** If $a$, $b$, $c$ are real numbers, then

  $$a(b + c) = ab + ac \qquad\text{and}\qquad ab + ac = a(b + c)$$

  The form on the left is used to multiply. The form on the right is used
  to factor.
{{< /callout >}}

So how do we use the Distributive Property to factor a polynomial? We find
the GCF of all the terms and write the polynomial as a product.

**Example.** Factor: $2x + 14$.

Find the GCF of $2x$ and $14$: it is $2$. Rewrite each term as a product
using the GCF:

$$2x + 14 = 2 \cdot x + 2 \cdot 7$$

Use the Distributive Property "in reverse" to factor the expression:

$$2(x + 7)$$

Check by multiplying the factors: $2(x + 7) = 2 \cdot x + 2 \cdot 7 = 2x + 14$. ✓

Notice that in the example above we used the word *factor* as both a noun
and a verb: $7$ is a *factor* of $14$ (noun), and we *factor* $2$ from
$2x + 14$ (verb).

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Factor the greatest common factor from a polynomial.**

  1. Find the GCF of all the terms of the polynomial.
  2. Rewrite each term as a product using the GCF.
  3. Use the Distributive Property "in reverse" to factor the expression.
  4. Check by multiplying the factors.
{{< /callout >}}

**Example.** Factor: $3a + 3$.

Find the GCF of $3a$ and $3$: it is $3$. Rewrite each term as a product
using the GCF, then factor:

$$3a + 3 = 3 \cdot a + 3 \cdot 1 = 3(a + 1)$$

Check: $3(a + 1) = 3 \cdot a + 3 \cdot 1 = 3a + 3$. ✓

The expressions in the next example have several factors in common.
Remember to write the GCF as the product of all the common factors.

**Example.** Factor: $12x - 60$.

Find the GCF of $12x$ and $60$: since $12x = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot x$ and
$60 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5$, the GCF is $2 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 = 12$.
Rewrite each term as a product using the GCF, then factor:

$$12x - 60 = 12 \cdot x - 12 \cdot 5 = 12(x - 5)$$

Check: $12(x - 5) = 12 \cdot x - 12 \cdot 5 = 12x - 60$. ✓

Now we'll factor the greatest common factor from a trinomial. We start by
finding the GCF of all three terms.

**Example.** Factor: $3y^2 + 6y + 9$.

Find the GCF of $3y^2$, $6y$, and $9$: it is $3$. Rewrite each term as a
product using the GCF, then factor:

$$3y^2 + 6y + 9 = 3 \cdot y^2 + 3 \cdot 2y + 3 \cdot 3 = 3(y^2 + 2y + 3)$$

Check: $3(y^2 + 2y + 3) = 3 \cdot y^2 + 3 \cdot 2y + 3 \cdot 3 = 3y^2 + 6y + 9$. ✓

In the next example, we factor a variable from a binomial.

**Example.** Factor: $6x^2 + 5x$.

Find the GCF of $6x^2$ and $5x$ and the math that goes with it: it is $x$.
Rewrite each term as a product, then factor:

$$6x^2 + 5x = x \cdot 6x + x \cdot 5 = x(6x + 5)$$

Check: $x(6x + 5) = x \cdot 6x + x \cdot 5 = 6x^2 + 5x$. ✓

When there are several common factors, as we'll see in the next two
examples, good organization and neat work helps!

**Example.** Factor: $4x^3 - 20x^2$.

Find the GCF of $4x^3$ and $20x^2$: since $4x^3 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot x \cdot x \cdot x$ and $20x^2 = 2 \cdot 2 \cdot 5 \cdot x \cdot x$, the GCF is $4x^2$. Rewrite each term and factor:

$$4x^3 - 20x^2 = 4x^2 \cdot x - 4x^2 \cdot 5 = 4x^2(x - 5)$$

Check: $4x^2(x - 5) = 4x^2 \cdot x - 4x^2 \cdot 5 = 4x^3 - 20x^2$. ✓

Find the GCF of $21y^2$ and $35y$: since $21y^2 = 3 \cdot 7 \cdot y \cdot y$
and $35y = 5 \cdot 7 \cdot y$, the GCF is $7y$. Rewrite each term and
factor:

$$21y^2 + 35y = 7y \cdot 3y + 7y \cdot 5 = 7y(3y + 5)$$

**Example.** Factor: $14x^3 + 8x^2 - 10x$.

Previously, we found the GCF of $14x^3$, $8x^2$, and $10x$ to be $2x$.
Rewrite each term using the GCF, then factor:

$$
14x^3 + 8x^2 - 10x = 2x \cdot 7x^2 + 2x \cdot 4x - 2x \cdot 5 = 2x(7x^2 + 4x - 5)
$$

Check: $2x(7x^2 + 4x - 5) = 2x \cdot 7x^2 + 2x \cdot 4x - 2x \cdot 5 = 14x^3 + 8x^2 - 10x$. ✓

When the leading coefficient — the coefficient of the first term — is
negative, we factor the negative out as part of the GCF.

**Example.** Factor: $-9y - 27$.

When the leading coefficient is negative, the GCF will be negative.
Ignoring the signs of the terms, we first find the GCF of $9y$ and $27$ is
$9$. Since the expression $-9y - 27$ has a negative leading coefficient, we
use $-9$ as the GCF. Rewrite each term using the GCF, then factor:

$$-9y - 27 = -9 \cdot y + (-9) \cdot 3 = -9(y + 3)$$

Check: $-9(y + 3) = -9 \cdot y + (-9) \cdot 3 = -9y - 27$. ✓

Pay close attention to the signs of the terms in the next example.

**Example.** Factor: $-4a^2 + 16a$.

The leading coefficient is negative, so the GCF will be negative. Ignoring
signs, the GCF of $4a^2$ and $16a$ is $4a$, so we use $-4a$ as the GCF.
Rewrite each term using the GCF, then factor:

$$-4a^2 + 16a = -4a \cdot a - (-4a) \cdot 4 = -4a(a - 4)$$

Check on your own by multiplying the factors.

## Key terms

**factoring** — splitting a product into the factors whose product it is;
the reverse of multiplying. **greatest common factor (GCF)** — of two or
more expressions, the largest expression that is a factor of all of them.
