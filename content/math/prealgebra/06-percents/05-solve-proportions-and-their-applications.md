## Use the definition of proportion

In Ratios and Rate we saw some ways ratios and rates are used in our daily
lives. When two ratios or rates are equal, the equation relating them is
called a **proportion**.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Proportion.** A proportion is an equation of the form
  $\tfrac{a}{b} = \tfrac{c}{d}$, where $b \neq 0, d \neq 0$.

  The proportion states two ratios or rates are equal. The proportion is
  read "$a$ is to $b$, as $c$ is to $d$".
{{< /callout >}}

The equation $\tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{4}{8}$ is a proportion because the two
fractions are equal. The proportion $\tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{4}{8}$ is read
"$1$ is to $2$ as $4$ is to $8$".

If we compare quantities with units, we have to be sure we are comparing
them in the right order. For example, in the proportion
$\tfrac{20\ \text{students}}{1\ \text{teacher}} =
\tfrac{60\ \text{students}}{3\ \text{teachers}}$ we compare the number of
students to the number of teachers. We put students in the numerators and
teachers in the denominators.

**Example.** Write each sentence as a proportion: (a) $3$ is to $7$ as
$15$ is to $35$. (b) $5$ hits in $8$ at-bats is the same as $30$ hits in
$48$ at-bats. (c) $\text{\textdollar}1.50$ for $6$ ounces is equivalent to
$\text{\textdollar}2.25$ for $9$ ounces.

(a) $\tfrac{3}{7} = \tfrac{15}{35}$.

(b) Write each fraction to compare hits to at-bats:
$\tfrac{\text{hits}}{\text{at-bats}} = \tfrac{\text{hits}}{\text{at-bats}}$,
so $\tfrac{5}{8} = \tfrac{30}{48}$.

(c) Write each fraction to compare dollars to ounces:
$\tfrac{\text{\textdollar}}{\text{ounces}} = \tfrac{\text{\textdollar}}{\text{ounces}}$,
so $\tfrac{1.50}{6} = \tfrac{2.25}{9}$.

Look at the proportions $\tfrac{1}{2} = \tfrac{4}{8}$ and
$\tfrac{2}{3} = \tfrac{6}{9}$. From our work with equivalent fractions we
know these equations are true. But how do we know if an equation is a
proportion with equivalent fractions if it contains fractions with larger
numbers?

To determine if a proportion is true, we find the **cross products** of
each proportion. To find the cross products, we multiply each denominator
with the opposite numerator (diagonally across the equal sign). The
results are called a cross product because of the cross formed. If, and
only if, the given proportion is true — that is, the two sides are equal
— then the cross products of a proportion will be equal.

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Cross products of a proportion.** For any proportion of the form
  $\tfrac{a}{b} = \tfrac{c}{d}$, where $b \neq 0, d \neq 0$, its cross
  products are equal: $a \cdot d = b \cdot c$.
{{< /callout >}}

Cross products can be used to test whether a proportion is true. To test
whether an equation makes a proportion, we find the cross products. If
they are both equal, we have a proportion.

**Example.** Determine whether each equation is a proportion:
(a) $\tfrac{4}{9} = \tfrac{12}{28}$ (b) $\tfrac{17.5}{37.5} = \tfrac{7}{15}$.

(a) Find the cross products: $28 \cdot 4 = 112$ and $9 \cdot 12 = 108$.
Since the cross products are not equal, $28 \cdot 4 \neq 9 \cdot 12$, the
equation is not a proportion.

(b) Find the cross products: $15 \cdot 17.5 = 262.5$ and
$37.5 \cdot 7 = 262.5$. Since the cross products are equal,
$15 \cdot 17.5 = 37.5 \cdot 7$, the equation is a proportion.

## Solve proportions

To solve a proportion containing a variable, we remember that the
proportion is an equation. All of the techniques we have used so far to
solve equations still apply. When the variable is in a numerator, we can
solve the proportion by multiplying both sides by the Least Common
Denominator (LCD), using the Multiplication Property of Equality.

**Example.** Solve: $\tfrac{x}{63} = \tfrac{4}{7}$.

To isolate $x$, multiply both sides by the LCD, $63$:
$63\left(\tfrac{x}{63}\right) = 63\left(\tfrac{4}{7}\right)$. Simplify:
$x = \tfrac{9 \cdot 7 \cdot 4}{7}$. Divide the common factors: $x = 36$.
Check: substitute $x = 36$ into the original proportion —
$\tfrac{36}{63} \overset{?}{=} \tfrac{4}{7}$; showing common factors,
$\tfrac{4 \cdot 9}{7 \cdot 9} \overset{?}{=} \tfrac{4}{7}$; simplify:
$\tfrac{4}{7} = \tfrac{4}{7}\ \checkmark$.

When the variable is in a denominator, we'll use the fact that the cross
products of a proportion are equal to solve the proportion. We can find
the cross products of the proportion and then set them equal. Then we
solve the resulting equation using our familiar techniques.

**Example.** Solve: $\tfrac{144}{a} = \tfrac{9}{4}$.

Find the cross products and set them equal: $4 \cdot 144 = a \cdot 9$.
Simplify: $576 = 9a$. Divide both sides by $9$: $\tfrac{576}{9} =
\tfrac{9a}{9}$. Simplify: $64 = a$. Check: substitute $a = 64$ —
$\tfrac{144}{64} \overset{?}{=} \tfrac{9}{4}$; showing common factors,
$\tfrac{9 \cdot 16}{4 \cdot 16} \overset{?}{=} \tfrac{9}{4}$; simplify:
$\tfrac{9}{4} = \tfrac{9}{4}\ \checkmark$.

## Solve applications using proportions

The strategy for solving applications that we have used earlier in this
chapter also works for proportions, since proportions are equations. When
we set up the proportion, we must make sure the units are correct — the
units in the numerators match and the units in the denominators match.

**Example.** When pediatricians prescribe acetaminophen to children, they
prescribe $5$ milliliters (ml) of acetaminophen for every $25$ pounds of
the child's weight. If Zoe weighs $80$ pounds, how many milliliters of
acetaminophen will her doctor prescribe?

Let $a =$ ml of acetaminophen. If $5$ ml is prescribed for every $25$
pounds, how much will be prescribed for $80$ pounds? Translate into a
proportion, being careful of the units:
$\tfrac{\text{ml}}{\text{pounds}} = \tfrac{\text{ml}}{\text{pounds}}$, so
$\tfrac{5}{25} = \tfrac{a}{80}$. Multiply both sides by $80$:
$80 \cdot \tfrac{5}{25} = 80 \cdot \tfrac{a}{80}$. Multiply and show
common factors: $\tfrac{16 \cdot 5 \cdot 5}{5 \cdot 5} = \tfrac{80a}{80}$.
Simplify: $16 = a$. Check: since $80$ is about $3$ times $25$, the
medicine should be about $3$ times $5$, and this checks out. The
pediatrician would prescribe $16$ ml of acetaminophen to Zoe.

**Example.** One brand of microwave popcorn has $120$ calories per
serving. A whole bag of this popcorn has $3.5$ servings. How many calories
are in a whole bag of this microwave popcorn?

Let $c =$ number of calories. If there are $120$ calories per serving, how
many calories are in a whole bag with $3.5$ servings? Translate into a
proportion: $\tfrac{120}{1} = \tfrac{c}{3.5}$. Multiply both sides by
$3.5$: $(3.5)\left(\tfrac{120}{1}\right) = (3.5)\left(\tfrac{c}{3.5}\right)$.
Multiply: $420 = c$. Check: since $3.5$ is between $3$ and $4$, the total
calories should be between $360$ ($3 \cdot 120$) and $480$
($4 \cdot 120$), and $420$ fits. The whole bag of microwave popcorn has
$420$ calories.

**Example.** Josiah went to Mexico for spring break and changed
$\text{\textdollar}325$ dollars into Mexican pesos. At that time, the
exchange rate had $\text{\textdollar}1$ U.S. equal to $12.54$ Mexican
pesos. How many Mexican pesos did he get for his trip?

Let $p =$ number of pesos. If $\text{\textdollar}1$ U.S. is equal to
$12.54$ Mexican pesos, then $\text{\textdollar}325$ is how many pesos?
Translate into a proportion: $\tfrac{1}{12.54} = \tfrac{325}{p}$. The
variable is in the denominator, so find the cross products and set them
equal: $p \cdot 1 = 12.54(325)$. Simplify: $p = 4{,}075.5$. Check: since
$\text{\textdollar}100$ would be $1{,}254$ pesos, and $\text{\textdollar}325$
is a little more than $3$ times this amount, this is reasonable. Josiah
has $4{,}075.5$ pesos for his spring break trip.

## Write percent equations as proportions

Previously, we solved percent equations by applying the properties of
equality. Some people prefer to solve percent equations by using the
proportion method. The proportion method for solving percent problems
involves a **percent proportion**. A percent proportion is an equation
where a percent is equal to an equivalent ratio.

For example, $60\% = \tfrac{60}{100}$, and we can simplify
$\tfrac{60}{100} = \tfrac{3}{5}$. Since the equation
$\tfrac{60}{100} = \tfrac{3}{5}$ shows a percent equal to an equivalent
ratio, we call it a percent proportion. Using the vocabulary we used
earlier for percent equations:

$$\frac{\text{amount}}{\text{base}} = \frac{\text{percent}}{100}$$

$$\frac{3}{5} = \frac{60}{100}$$

{{< callout type="info" >}}
  **Percent proportion.** The amount is to the base as the percent is to
  $100$:

  $$\frac{\text{amount}}{\text{base}} = \frac{\text{percent}}{100}$$
{{< /callout >}}

If we restate the problem in the words of a proportion, it may be easier
to set up: *the amount is to the base as the percent is to one hundred*.
We could also say: *the amount out of the base is the same as the percent
out of one hundred*. First we will practice translating into a percent
proportion. Later, we'll solve the proportion.

**Example.** Translate to a proportion: What number is $75\%$ of $90$?

If you look for the word "of", it may help you identify the base.
Identify the parts of the percent proportion: "what number" is the
amount, $75\%$ is the percent, and $90$ (following "of") is the base.
Restate as a proportion: what number out of $90$ is the same as $75$ out
of $100$? Set up the proportion, letting $n =$ number: $\tfrac{n}{90} =
\tfrac{75}{100}$.

## Translate and solve percent proportions

Now that we have written percent equations as proportions, we are ready to
solve the equations.

**Example.** Translate and solve using proportions: What number is $45\%$
of $80$?

Identify the parts of the percent proportion: "what number" is the
amount, $45\%$ is the percent, $80$ is the base. Restate as a proportion:
what number out of $80$ is the same as $45$ out of $100$? Set up the
proportion, letting $n =$ number: $\tfrac{n}{80} = \tfrac{45}{100}$. Find
the cross products and set them equal: $100 \cdot n = 80 \cdot 45$.
Simplify: $100n = 3{,}600$. Divide both sides by $100$: $n = 36$. Check:
$45$ is a little less than half of $100$, and $36$ is a little less than
half of $80$, so this is reasonable. $36$ is $45\%$ of $80$.

In the next example, the percent is more than $100$, which is more than
one whole. So the unknown number will be more than the base.

**Example.** Translate and solve using proportions: $125\%$ of $25$ is
what number?

Identify the parts of the percent proportion: $125\%$ is the percent,
$25$ is the base, "what number" is the amount. Restate as a proportion:
what number out of $25$ is the same as $125$ out of $100$? Set up the
proportion: $\tfrac{n}{25} = \tfrac{125}{100}$. Find the cross products
and set them equal: $100 \cdot n = 25 \cdot 125$. Simplify:
$100n = 3{,}125$. Divide both sides by $100$: $n = 31.25$. Check: $125$
is more than $100$ and $31.25$ is more than $25$, so this is reasonable.
$125\%$ of $25$ is $31.25$.

Percents with decimals and money are also used in proportions.

**Example.** Translate and solve: $6.5\%$ of what number is
$\text{\textdollar}1.56$?

Identify the parts of the percent proportion: $6.5\%$ is the percent,
"what number" is the base, $\text{\textdollar}1.56$ is the amount.
Restate as a proportion: $\text{\textdollar}1.56$ out of what number is
the same as $6.5$ out of $100$? Set up the proportion, letting $n =$
number: $\tfrac{1.56}{n} = \tfrac{6.5}{100}$. Find the cross products and
set them equal: $100(1.56) = n \cdot 6.5$. Simplify: $156 = 6.5n$. Divide
both sides by $6.5$: $\tfrac{156}{6.5} = \tfrac{6.5n}{6.5}$. Simplify:
$24 = n$. Check: $6.5\%$ is a small amount and $\text{\textdollar}1.56$ is
much less than $\text{\textdollar}24$, so this is reasonable. $6.5\%$ of
$\text{\textdollar}24$ is $\text{\textdollar}1.56$.

## Key terms

**proportion** — an equation of the form $\tfrac{a}{b} = \tfrac{c}{d}$,
where $b \neq 0, d \neq 0$, stating that two ratios or rates are equal.
**cross products** — the products found by multiplying each denominator
of a proportion with the opposite numerator; equal cross products
indicate a true proportion. **percent proportion** — a proportion of the
form $\tfrac{\text{amount}}{\text{base}} = \tfrac{\text{percent}}{100}$.
